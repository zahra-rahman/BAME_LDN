Sexual Assault: Stigma and Disproportionality

Anonymous

>
Scroll Down

Following the death of George Floyd...

There has been a rapid and unprecedented surge in support for movements such as Black Lives Matter as well as an increased awareness of the power of the police force (and the abuse of said power), particularly in the United States (although the issue is not contained to that one country).



Realities of Abuse of Power

Social media has always been a platform for sharing information but the type of information shared has become far more educational than ever before. The topic of crime, and how we should deal with it, has now been pushed to the forefront of the public eye.

It’s clear that the police have an overabundance of power which they have frequently used to abuse the general public (specifically those within minority groups). For example, data from the FBI’s Supplementary Homicide Report (from 2012) highlighted that black people compose 13% of the US population yet they account for a wildly disproportionate 31% of all people killed by American police officers.

In the UK, 163 people have died in police custody or following it in the past 10 years (according to the Independent Office for Police Conduct). Of those 163, 23 were from BAME groups.
Only 11% of the UK population is BAME (according to the 2011 United Kingdom Census) yet they made up 14% of the deaths as found in this study. Unfortunately, this UK study only covered statistics related to police custody meaning that the total number of police killings is certainly higher (and I would suspect that the disproportionality between the treatment of BAME individuals as compared to others is also much larger).

Sex as a Taboo

However, one type of crime which hasn’t been highlighted as strongly as police brutality is sexual crime. The topic of rape and sexual assault as a whole is incredibly sensitive, most likely stemming from the fact that sex is a taboo talking point in our society.

The fact that sex is a topic which is actively avoided leads to a large number of issues ranging from smaller problems (such as confusion regarding intercourse itself) to larger issues (such as misinformation regarding consent).
There have been numerous studies regarding not only the perpetrators of rape but also the victims of such an assault and the physical and/or psychological impact that sexual assault creates.

Victims, Rapists and Convictions

Analysis of United States data derived from Justice Department National Crime Victimization Surveys (from 2010 to 2014) by RAINN (the Rape, Abuse and Incest National Network) discovered only 31% of rape incidents are reported to the police Furthermore, the study found that a staggeringly low 0.6% of convicted rapists are incarcerated.

Contrastingly, analyses of Justice Department National Crime Victimization Surveys (from 2009 to 2012) found that the impacts of sexual assault on the victims are far more damaging. 75% of victims experienced significant problems with work, family and friends, 30-50% suffered from depression and 30-45% contemplated suicide (with 5-15% attempting suicide).

Statistics found by the UK Home Office found that a shocking 98% of alleged sexual assailants (reported to the police) face no consequences. The number of sex crimes recorded by UK police rose by 9 per cent between 2017 and 2018 yet the number of prosecuted sexual offences (in the UK) dropped by over 4,000 in the same period.

Unfortunately, the real number of sexual assault incidents is undoubtedly much larger than these statistics suggest due to the fact that over two thirds of sexual assault incidents aren’t reported (as highlighted by the aforementioned analysis of US statistics by RAINN).

Shocking Stats Explained...

There are a number of reasons behind the horrifically low rate of convictions of sexual assailants, one of which is the dismissive attitudes of judges in courts of law. For example, a BBC News report published in February 2020 detailed the story of a British woman who objected to her former partner being granted time with their son (due to the fact that he had previously assaulted and raped her).

Judge Robin Tolson (who presided over the hearing) stated that the woman "was not in any sense pinned down", she "could easily, physically, have made life harder" for the man and it "did not constitute rape". The woman later lost the legal battle against her partner. This story only scratches the surface of the outdated, bigoted and downright damaging attitudes which are so frequently found within courts of law and within others in powerful positions (such as US president Donald Trump, who dismissed the high rates of sexual assault in the military in May 2013 by stating, “What did these geniuses expect when they put men & women together?”).

Moreover, whilst all 50 US states deem rape by a police officer to be illegal, 35 of those states lack laws which expressly define sex between police officers and detainees as non-consensual. This inevitably and sadly leads to people being sexually assaulted by police officers since a victim’s claims will almost certainly be dismissed. This is not only shown by the already low rate of assailant prosecution but also because police officers possess more power than an average citizen, especially due to the lack of these fundamental laws, thus can abuse said power to gain a far greater influence within a court of law.

Finally, I would suspect that the blatant disproportionality between the treatment of BAME individuals and others mentioned throughout this post makes them more likely to be targets and victims of sexual assault by police officers (since the likelihood of an officer being convicted from a claim made by a BAME individual is significantly lower).

Conclusion

In conclusion, sexual assault is a far-reaching and worsening issue which is fueled by stigma and the historic abuse of power and the legal system. Removing the stigma surrounding sex, sexual assault and other related topics (a responsibility which falls upon us as individuals) as well as increasing the priority of education about consent in schools are critical steps in the journey to raising the percentage of reported sexual assaults and, more crucially, raising the percentage of convicted sexual assailants.
